Once our choice for chestnut was done, we had to find a suitable block and also get it on location where we would process it. This was a real challenge for us, which we only could realize through the use of different techniques.

CHESTNUT WOOD

Chestnut wood characterizes itself as a light wood that is also soft in treatment. Ideal for creating art and turning residuals into an alternative.

CHISELING

In order to eliminate the wood with delicacy, the hammer and chisel were used after the chainsaw to give the structure an even better shape.

CRAFTS 

In order to obtain more relief in the prototype, we started experimenting. On the basis of coincidences we came up with results that we did not dare to predict in advance. The image above is the result of this. 

REPRODUCTION

The three shadows that appealed us the most, we chose and started working with it. This resulted in new insights into our preliminary designs.

MASSIVE

After several hours the preliminary designs already consisted of flat volumes, right angles and superficial notches. But how would the chestnut wood react to organic forms, rounded corners and fully perforated cavities.



Meraki owes its appearance to the Japanese technique known as ‘ Shou-sugi-ban’ a process where wood gets superficially burnt thus it increases in beauty and durability. 

.




The powerful fire leaves its marks and offers resistance to the natural elements at the same time. Although the wood gets burnt it doesn’t harm its natural structure. 

DEVELOPER




The finishing of the object had to be profiled in black. The surface of the carbonised block obtained a perfect imitation of a shadow shape and its diversity of color nuances.
